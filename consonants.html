The Alphabet: Consonants

Korean consonants are split into several groups: Regular, Aspirated, and Double.

Regular Consonants

Let’s learn the regular consonants first, which make up 9/14 Korean consonants:

ㅂ ㄱ ㄷ ㅎ ㅈ ㄹ ㅁ ㄴ ㅅ
b g d h j r/l m n s

The Korean letters on the top row correspond to the English letters below. Remember, in Korean, "R" and "L" are represented by the same letter ㄹ. In some words, ㄹ produces the one sound or the other. There is no solid rule to distinguish these instances, so you will gradually learn instances pronunciations in various vocabulary.

Aspirated Consonants

Aspirated Consonants make up 4/14 Korean consonants in the alphabet. Pronounce the sounds of “B, D, G, J” with more force. Notice how you start to hear “P, T, K, and Ch” respectively.

If you’re still unsure, practice saying “cheap jeep”, “grape crepe”, "dime time”, or “bow pow.”

In Korean, there are four more letters representing the sounds "P, T, K, and Ch". Here's the twist: they look very similar to the letters for "B, D, G, and J"! Let's take a look below:

Aspirated Consonants and their Regular Consonant Counterparts

B ㅂ P ㅍ
D ㄷ T ㅌ
G ㄱ K ㅋ
J ㅈ Ch ㅊ

See how the letters for aspirated consonants are similar to their regular consonant counterparts? We have just learned 13/14 consonants!

The video below provides pronunciations for the 13 consonants we have learned so far. Memorize these letters and their sounds before moving on.

Note: There is one last consonant which is explained right below.

Special Consonant:ㅇ

Finally, the last consonant of the 14 Korean consonants: ㅇ (no, this is not the English letter "O").

This consonant is very special because it has 2 functions. The letter ㅇis silent when it starts a syllable since it serves as a placeholder, but when ㅇ ends a word (only comes after a vowel), it becomes the “-ng” sound like in “running”.
Note: no vowel can start a syllable/word by itself, it MUST have ㅇ attached in the front. Example: (유 is correct, ᅲ is incorrect). However, don’t worry about this just yet!

Double Consonants

No new letters are introduced here, because we have already learned all 14 of the 14 Korean consonants ( yay). Double consonants are simply a combination of REGULAR consonants that are treated like individual letters. When you see double consonants, also known as strong consonants, simply pronounce the letter more a little stronger. Be sure that you aren't forcing so much sound that you're turning the double consonants into Aspirated Consonants!

Don't be intimidated. Double Consonants are literally doubled Regular consonants, and there are only 5 of them!

Double Consonants Chart

ㄸ ㅉ ㄲ ㅃ ㅆ
dd jj gg bb ss

Let's practice pronouncing and understanding the concept of Double Consonants some more. Imagine you are yelling "BUS!" or "GEORGE" to a friend. Notice how the beginning consonants were emphasized in sound? You didn't aspirate the consonants, or else it would've sounded like "PUS!" and "CHEORGE".

Essentially, this is the concept of Double Consonants, a feature truly unique to the Korean Language!
